The eight-limb framework is one way to think about awareness. It is not the only way, and it would be irresponsible to write three thousand words about contemplative neuroscience without mentioning that the West has been arguing about what consciousness actually *is* for at least 2,500 years.<SideNote>The historical claim is a little casual. Parmenides and Heraclitus were arguing about being and becoming in the fifth century BCE, which is contemporary with the early Upaniṣads and predates Patañjali by a century or two. The Greek and Indian traditions appear to have developed independently — Alexander's invasion was still a few hundred years off — though there are intriguing pre-Alexandrian trade-route possibilities that scholars argue about. None of this affects the present essay, but the parallel timing is worth noting.</SideNote> The argument is still going. This survey introduces the major positions, with enough specificity that a partisan of each could recognize themselves in the description.

The honest framing first. Consciousness has resisted explanation for a long time, and the disagreement is not because some theories are obviously wrong. It is because each major theory captures something the others miss, and each makes predictions that have not yet been decisively tested against the others. The 2023 COGITATE adversarial collaboration<Cite id="cogitate-2023" /> — the most ambitious head-to-head test of two leading theories to date — concluded with both camps claiming partial vindication and the field arguing about what the result actually showed.<SideNote>This is, I should say, exactly what good science looks like when the questions are hard. The disappointment some commentators expressed at COGITATE 2023 was misplaced. We do not get to skip the long slow argument; we just get to make it slightly more rigorous.</SideNote>

So: nine frameworks, each given a fair hearing, with the implications for contemplative practice spelled out at the end of each.

## 1. Global Workspace Theory

The current center of empirical gravity. Bernard Baars proposed Global Workspace Theory in 1988<Cite id="baars-1988" /> as a cognitive architecture; Stanislas Dehaene and colleagues developed it into the Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) framework, with specific neural commitments and a now-well-characterized empirical signature.<Cite id="dehaene-2014" /><Cite id="mashour-2020" />

The central claim: consciousness happens when information is *broadcast* across a network of fronto-parietal cortical areas, becoming available to working memory, decision-making, language, and intentional behavior. The metaphor is theatrical — a workspace, a stage on which winning content is illuminated and announced to the rest of the brain. Most cognitive processing happens in specialized modules below the threshold of awareness; consciousness is what happens when one of those modules' outputs is selected for global broadcast.<SideNote>The theatrical metaphor sometimes gets criticized as a homunculus argument — who is watching the show? Baars and Dehaene are aware of this and respond that the workspace is the broadcast itself, not an audience. Whether you find that satisfying probably tracks closely with whether you find higher-order theories satisfying.</SideNote>

The empirical signature is striking. In experiments contrasting consciously perceived stimuli with unconscious ones (matched for everything except whether they reach awareness), conscious perception is associated with a late, widespread ignition of fronto-parietal cortex around 200–300 ms after stimulus onset. Unconscious processing produces earlier, more localized activity that does not propagate. The GNW prediction is that the late frontal activity is the signature of consciousness; without it, no awareness.

For contemplative practice, this framework has implications. Dhāraṇā — sustained selective attention — is the practice of deliberately controlling what gets broadcast. The executive network does the selection; the workspace is the substrate the selection works through.<SideNote>Lutz and Slagter's attentional-blink work in long-term meditators<Cite id="lutz-slagter-2008" /> can be read as a GNW result: meditation training improves the brain's ability to bring a second target to ignition within the refractory period after a first. The workspace cycles faster.</SideNote> Samādhi, on a GNW reading, is hard to characterize — perhaps a state in which the workspace is occupied by a content too subtle to be reported, or a state in which the broadcast loop temporarily decouples. Honest answer: GNW does not have a clean story about deep contemplative states. It was not designed to.

## 2. Integrated Information Theory

The bold mathematical alternative. Giulio Tononi has been refining IIT since 2004; the most cited modern versions are IIT 3.0<Cite id="tononi-2016" /> and the accessible "Provisional Manifesto"<Cite id="tononi-2008" />.

IIT starts from the experience side. It identifies a small number of axioms that, on Tononi's analysis, any conscious experience necessarily satisfies — intrinsicality, composition, information, integration, exclusion. It then works backward, asking what physical structure could instantiate something with those properties. The answer is a quantity called Φ (phi): the amount of integrated information a system possesses, where integration means the information is more than the sum of the system's parts.

A system is conscious to the degree that it has nonzero Φ. *How* conscious is determined by *how much* Φ. *What it is conscious of* — the specific quality of experience — is determined by the structure of the integrated information, which Tononi calls the "qualia structure" or "conceptual structure."

This is a sweeping theory. It has consequences. IIT predicts that systems with high integration but limited differentiation (a cerebellum, perhaps) have very low Φ despite their structural complexity — which matches the clinical observation that cerebellar damage doesn't impair consciousness the way cortical damage does. It also predicts that systems with the right pattern of integrated information could be conscious even if they look nothing like brains; some commentators have read this as IIT implying a kind of cautious panpsychism, which Tononi has both accepted and resisted depending on which paper you read.<SideNote>The IIT-panpsychism connection is real but careful. IIT does not claim everything is conscious; it claims that any system with nonzero Φ has some degree of experience. Most things — thermostats, rocks, dust — have Φ near zero. The interesting claim is that consciousness is not exclusively biological; it is a structural feature of certain integrated systems, biological or otherwise.</SideNote>

The COGITATE collaboration tested IIT-derived predictions about posterior "hot zones" of consciousness — and got partial support. Posterior cortex was reliably involved; the timing predictions were closer to IIT's expectations than to GNW's; the exact spatial predictions held up only partially.<Cite id="cogitate-2023" />

For contemplative practice, IIT is interesting because it makes samādhi tractable as a research question. Deep contemplative states should, on IIT's account, be characterized by altered Φ — possibly higher, possibly lower, depending on whether the experience is described as more or less differentiated than ordinary awareness. The framework gives the contemplative neuroscientist a number to chase. Whether the number means what the framework says it means is the open question.

## 3. Higher-Order Theories

The philosophical alternative. David Rosenthal's 1986 formulation<Cite id="rosenthal-1986" />, developed at book length two decades later<Cite id="rosenthal-2005" />, holds that a mental state is conscious when it is the target of a higher-order representation — a representation *about* the first-order state. You are not conscious of red simply because you have a state representing red. You are conscious of red because you also have a state representing that you have a state representing red.

The HOT family branches: actualist HOT (Rosenthal — the higher-order state must actually exist), dispositional HOT (Carruthers — it must merely be potentially available), higher-order perception (Lycan — the higher-order representation is perception-like), same-order representational (Kriegel — the first-order and higher-order components merge into one integrated state).<SideNote>This is exactly the kind of taxonomy that gives philosophy of mind its reputation. Each variant exists because the previous one had a problem. Same-order theories exist because actualist HOT has the misrepresentation problem (what happens if the higher-order state misrepresents its target?); dispositional theories exist because actualist HOT seemed to predict that no thought is conscious until you think a thought about it, which is implausible. The arguments are real; the proliferation is also real.</SideNote>

The empirical case for higher-order theories rests on findings that prefrontal cortex activity tracks subjective awareness more reliably than it tracks objective task performance.<Cite id="lau-rosenthal-2011" /> Lau's argument: if PFC activity is what makes a state conscious, then we should be able to dissociate awareness from performance, and we can. There are conditions in which subjects perform a task above chance without reporting awareness; there are conditions in which subjects report awareness despite chance performance. PFC tracks the report, not the performance.

For contemplative practice, higher-order theories have specific implications. *Svādhyāya* — self-study, the niyama of metacognition — is, on this account, the deliberate cultivation of the higher-order representational capacity that makes states conscious in the first place. Meditation that strengthens metacognition is, on an HOT reading, meditation that strengthens consciousness itself.<SideNote>This is a strong claim and not one I would endorse without caveats. The empirical case for HOT-related PFC findings is real but contested; the inference from "meditation improves metacognition" to "meditation strengthens consciousness" jumps a few terms in the theoretical chain. Hold loosely.</SideNote>

## 4. Recurrent Processing Theory

Victor Lamme's framework<Cite id="lamme-2006" /><Cite id="lamme-2010" /> takes the empirically opposite stance from GNW and HOT. Local recurrent processing in posterior sensory cortex — feedback loops between visual cortical areas, for instance — is sufficient for *phenomenal* consciousness. The fronto-parietal broadcast that GNW emphasizes is necessary for access and report, not for experience itself.

The argument leans on Block's P-consciousness / A-consciousness distinction<Cite id="block-1995" />. Lamme argues that GNW and HOT have conflated two things — the having of an experience and the cognitive availability of an experience — and that the former does not require the latter. The brain experiences much more than it reports.

The empirical evidence: brief stimuli that the subject denies seeing nonetheless produce reliable recurrent activity in early visual cortex, and that activity has the temporal structure characteristic of feedback rather than feedforward processing. Lamme reads this as P-consciousness without A-consciousness. GNW reads it as unconscious processing that happens to produce recurrent dynamics. The dispute is still open.

For contemplative practice, Lamme's framework has an unexpected implication. The pratyāhāra limb — sensory withdrawal — works on thalamic gating, which modulates exactly the feedforward signals Lamme thinks are insufficient for phenomenal consciousness anyway. The phenomenal richness of inner experience during deep meditation, which traditional practitioners describe as undiminished even when external input is gated, would fit Lamme's framework: the recurrent processing remains, the broadcast capacity remains, only the access-style report changes.

## 5. Predictive Processing and Active Inference

The framework I find most useful for the present project, and the one I have leaned on hardest in the synthesis paper.

Karl Friston's free-energy principle<Cite id="friston-2010" /> proposes that the brain is a prediction machine. It generates models of what its inputs will be — both exteroceptive (the world) and interoceptive (the body) — and updates those models based on the difference between prediction and actual signal (prediction error). The drive to minimize prediction error explains learning, perception, action, and, on some readings, experience itself.

Andy Clark's *Whatever Next?*<Cite id="clark-2013" /> brought predictive processing to a wide audience in cognitive science. Anil Seth's *Being You*<Cite id="seth-2021" /> brought it to a wide audience generally. The Seth-Friston paper on active interoceptive inference<Cite id="seth-friston-2016" /> is the document I have leaned on most directly for the prāṇāyāma material — interoception as the brain's predictive model of body state, updated by error signals carried up by the vagus and other afferent pathways.

The framework absorbs many problems that other theories struggle with. Perception is constructive — the felt world is the model, refined by error. Emotion is interoceptive inference — felt states are predictions about body state. Mental disorders, on this account, are disorders of prediction-error processing (autism as too-precise priors, psychosis as too-loose priors, depression as priors that are too pessimistic to be updated by good news).

For contemplative practice, predictive processing has implications across the entire eight-limb stack. Prāṇāyāma changes the interoceptive prediction-error landscape directly. Dhāraṇā is a top-down control of which prediction streams attention attends to. Pratyāhāra reduces the sensory signal that drives prediction-error updates. Dhyāna and samādhi, on this framework, might be characterized as states in which the high-level priors that normally generate the sense of a separate self temporarily relax — REBUS-style.<Cite id="carhart-harris-2019" />

## 6. The Entropic Brain and REBUS

Robin Carhart-Harris's entropic-brain hypothesis<Cite id="carhart-harris-2014" /> emerged from imaging studies of psilocybin<Cite id="carhart-harris-2012" /> and produced a result that was initially baffling: psychedelics *decrease* brain activity, not increase it. The decrease was most pronounced in the default mode network — the same network that meditation studies were independently finding to be quieted in experienced practitioners.<Cite id="brewer-2011" />

The entropic-brain hypothesis: consciousness is graded by the entropy (disorder, flexibility, unpredictability) of neural activity. "Secondary states" — adult waking awareness, normal cognition, reality-testing — are low-entropy states with the DMN holding things together. "Primary states" — psychedelic experience, REM dreaming, early development, deep meditation — are high-entropy states in which the DMN's constraint relaxes and a wider range of mental contents become accessible.<SideNote>Carhart-Harris is explicit that primary and secondary states are not a value hierarchy. Primary states have advantages (flexibility, novelty, creative recombination) and costs (instability, vulnerability to disorganization). Secondary states have advantages (reality-testing, executive control) and costs (rigidity, defensive narrowness). Healthy adult cognition involves the right balance for the situation; pathology, on this view, is often imbalance.</SideNote>

The REBUS extension<Cite id="carhart-harris-2019" /> — RElaxed Beliefs Under pSychedelics — unifies the entropic brain with predictive processing. Psychedelics relax high-level priors. The top-down constraints that normally shape perception become looser. Bottom-up signal carries more weight. The model has less to fight against in updating, which is why psychedelic experiences feel both vivid (high prediction error) and meaningful (rapid model revision).

The framework predicts something specific about deep contemplative states. If samādhi involves quieting of the same networks (DMN, fronto-parietal control) that psychedelics relax, and if the phenomenology overlaps (ego dissolution, unity experiences, time distortion), then meditation and psychedelics might be reaching similar attractor states via different routes. The empirical evidence is partial but suggestive.<SideNote>"Partial but suggestive" is doing real work here. The overlap is real — Newberg<Cite id="newberg-2010" />, Griffiths<Cite id="griffiths-2006" />, and the Carhart-Harris group have all noted the similarity between high-dose psychedelic experiences and reports from advanced meditators. The overlap is also incomplete in ways that matter to practitioners. Meditation produces durable changes in baseline function; psychedelics typically do not, although the recent psilocybin trials are beginning to suggest that single high-dose experiences may produce lasting trait-level changes in some subjects. The full story is not in yet.</SideNote>

## 7. Attention Schema Theory

Michael Graziano's framework<Cite id="graziano-webb-2015" /><Cite id="graziano-2019" />: consciousness is the brain's simplified model of its own attentional processes. The attention schema is to attention what the body schema is to motor control — an internal representation that lets the system monitor and control itself without needing to track the underlying mechanism in detail.

The theory makes a specific phenomenological claim: when you report being aware of something, you are reporting the contents of your attention schema. The schema is necessarily simplified and partly inaccurate (it does not represent attention's underlying neural mechanism, only its functional contours), and that gap between the schema and the actual mechanism is what makes consciousness *feel* like something mysterious. There is no ghost in the machine, but the machine has a model of itself that does not include the machinery — and the model is what we call awareness.

For contemplative practice, AST has a specific implication. Meditation that refines metacognitive monitoring — most contemplative practice, frankly — is refining the attention schema itself. Long-term practitioners should have more accurate, more granular, more responsive attention schemas. The phenomenological reports of advanced meditators (a quality of "knowing the mind from inside" that is hard to describe) might be what an accurate attention schema feels like from the inside.

## 8. Embodied and Enactive Cognition

The framework that has done the most explicit work to bridge contemplative traditions and cognitive science. *The Embodied Mind* (1991)<Cite id="varela-thompson-rosch-1991" />, written by Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch, argued that mind is not in the head — it is in the dynamic loop between organism and world. The enactive position rejected the computational metaphor that dominated 1980s cognitive science and proposed instead that cognition emerges from embodied action.

Varela was a Chilean biologist trained at Harvard with Maturana, but he was also a long-term Buddhist practitioner. The book engaged Madhyamaka Buddhism explicitly. The argument: the deepest insight of Buddhist philosophy — that the self is empty of inherent existence, constructed in dependence on conditions — is structurally identical to the enactive claim that cognition is not produced by a self-contained mind but enacted in the loop between body and world.<SideNote>Whether the structural identity is real or whether Varela was projecting his Buddhism onto the cognitive science is a fair question. Thompson's later work<Cite id="thompson-2007" /><Cite id="thompson-2014" /> has done the more careful work of separating what the structural argument actually shows from what would be hopeful overclaim.</SideNote>

Thompson's *Mind in Life*<Cite id="thompson-2007" /> developed the enactive program to its mature form. *Waking, Dreaming, Being*<Cite id="thompson-2014" /> is probably the most philosophically careful book ever written about contemplative neuroscience — it engages samādhi and non-dual awareness without either inflating them or dismissing them, and it is the book I have learned from most directly.

For contemplative practice, the enactive framework is the obvious home. Āsana cultivates the body that does the cognition. Prāṇāyāma works on the autonomic loops that constitute much of felt experience. The contemplative traditions, on this reading, were always doing enactive cognitive science — they just called it something else.

## 9. The hard problem, briefly

David Chalmers's 1995 paper<Cite id="chalmers-1995" /> introduced the framing that has structured the philosophical conversation ever since. The "easy" problems of consciousness are explaining specific functions — how the brain produces discrimination, integration, report, sleep-wake cycles, attention control. The "hard" problem is explaining why there is something it is like to be a brain at all — why the functions are accompanied by subjective experience rather than running in the dark.

Thomas Nagel's earlier paper<Cite id="nagel-1974" /> made the same point with bats. Whatever bat neuroscience eventually tells us about echolocation, no amount of objective description will tell us *what it is like* to echolocate. The subjective character of experience is, on Nagel's account, fundamentally not the kind of thing third-person description can capture.

Thirty years on, the hard problem is exactly as hard as Chalmers said it was. The empirical theories — GNW, IIT, HOT, AST — make progress on the easy problems. They explain what consciousness *does* and *how* it is implemented. They do not explain why doing-and-implementing produces felt experience.

Thomas Metzinger's *Being No One*<Cite id="metzinger-2003" /> tries to deflate the hard problem by arguing that the self that seems to need explaining is itself a model — there is no one whose felt experience needs to be accounted for, just a phenomenal self-model that the brain runs. The argument is interesting and not, in the end, fully successful; the question of why the model is accompanied by phenomenal qualities at all remains.

The contemplative traditions have something to offer this conversation that they did not have in 1995: populations of practitioners who have spent decades exploring the state-space the philosophers are arguing about. Whether their reports constitute *data* on the hard problem is itself a hard question. They might, at minimum, constrain what an adequate theory has to explain. Any theory of consciousness that cannot account for the structural features of trained contemplative experience — the dissolution of the subject-object distinction, the cultivability of awareness, the layered architecture the eight limbs describes — is a theory missing some of the data.

## Where this leaves us

Nine frameworks, nine partial accounts, no clear winner. This is not, I want to insist, a failure of consciousness science. It is what hard problems look like in the middle of being worked on. Each theory captures something real. Each theory misses something the others see.

The synthesis paper takes a position. The position is that predictive processing, with its active-inference account of interoception, gives the cleanest translation between contemplative phenomenology and modern neuroscience; that the entropic-brain framework usefully extends this to characterize deep contemplative states as DMN-relaxed, prior-loosened conditions structurally akin to psychedelic states; that the embodied-enactive program is the philosophical home for taking contemplative traditions seriously as a source of data; and that the hard problem of consciousness will not be dissolved by any of this, although it may be partially clarified.

A different position is defensible. A higher-order theorist would emphasize metacognitive cultivation as the central contemplative move. A Global Workspace theorist would emphasize attentional control of broadcast. An IIT theorist would emphasize the structural changes in integrated information across states. The contemplative traditions are large enough to be read through any of these frames; they make different empirical predictions, all of which are in principle testable, and none of which has been decisively tested.

To summarize plainly: the eight limbs describe a substrate. Modern Western theories of consciousness disagree about what that substrate is doing when it produces awareness. The disagreement is not a flaw in the survey; it is the survey.
