*Dhāraṇā* is the sixth limb. Patañjali defines it at *Yoga Sūtras* 3.1 as the "binding of consciousness to a single locus" (*deśa-bandhaḥ cittasya dhāraṇā*). The object can be the breath, a mantra, an image, a sensation, a candle flame. What matters is the *sustaining* — the willingness to bring attention back, again and again, when it wanders.

The cognitive operation is simple to describe and famously hard to do. The mind wanders within seconds. The practitioner notices the wandering, releases it, and returns. The whole limb is the return.

## The executive control network

Modern cognitive neuroscience has independently mapped the substrate of sustained selective attention. The executive control network — dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), posterior parietal cortex (PPC), and the connections between them — is what does the work.<Cite id="lutz-slagter-2008" /> Tasks that require sustained attention reliably activate this network; damage to it produces characteristic attentional deficits; long-term training in attention-demanding skills produces measurable structural changes in it.

The salience network — anchored in the anterior insula and anterior cingulate cortex — does the switching: it decides, moment by moment, whether the default mode network or the executive control network should be in charge. Long-term practitioners show altered salience-network activity that suggests they have, in effect, *more refined control* over which network is dominant at which time. Dhāraṇā is the practice of pulling the system into executive dominance and holding it there.

Lazar's 2005 imaging work<Cite id="lazar-2005" /> showed cortical thickening in prefrontal regions in long-term meditators. Follow-up work has consistently found that attention-focused meditation styles — concentration practices, mantra practices, breath-attention practices — produce changes in the executive control network specifically. The structural changes are real, replicated, and broadly consistent with the cognitive-science expectation that sustained training in an attention-demanding skill thickens the cortex implementing that skill.

The performance evidence is also clear. The Lutz-Slagter work on the attentional blink in long-term meditators<Cite id="lutz-slagter-2008" /> found that intensive attention training reduces the "blink" — the normal failure to perceive a second target shortly after a first target in rapid sequences. Practitioners can sustain attention across intervals where most people's attention briefly fails. This is not metaphorical; it is a measurable improvement on a standard cognitive task that practitioners have never specifically practiced.

## Focused attention versus open monitoring

Lutz, Slagter, Dunne, and Davidson's 2008 framework paper<Cite id="lutz-slagter-2008" /> introduced a distinction that has shaped subsequent meditation neuroscience: *focused attention* (FA) practices, in which attention is bound to a specific object, versus *open monitoring* (OM) practices, in which attention is non-reactively aware of whatever arises. The distinction maps cleanly onto Patañjali's framework: dhāraṇā is the prototypical FA practice; dhyāna, as we will see on the next page, has more in common with OM.

The neural signatures differ. FA practices in long-term practitioners show robust activation of the executive control network and progressively reduced activation as practice deepens — the practitioner needs less effort to sustain the same attention. OM practices show different signatures, with default-mode-network changes that we will treat in the dhyāna page. The early phase of dhāraṇā in beginners shows what cognitive science would expect: heavy DLPFC engagement, considerable subjective effort, frequent attention failures.

This is, I think, the limb most directly approachable through Western cognitive science. The vocabulary maps. The mechanisms are well-characterized. The training effect is robust enough to be visible across many independent studies. Dhāraṇā is the limb Western attention research was already studying when it noticed that long-term meditators were doing it better than most people.

## Why this limb is the easiest to study

Of all eight limbs, dhāraṇā is the one Western cognitive science was already studying when it stumbled into yoga. The cognitive science of attention is a mature field. It has good paradigms (the attentional blink, the Stroop task, sustained vigilance, the various flanker tasks), good neural correlates, and good intervention studies on populations from children with ADHD to elite athletes.

When that field met the yogic tradition's long-term attention practitioners, both gained. The yogic tradition gained access to instruments that could measure what it had been training for centuries. The cognitive science gained a population that had done the training to a depth no controlled trial could replicate — practitioners with twenty, thirty, fifty thousand hours of attention practice. The Davidson lab at Wisconsin made much of this collaboration. The findings have been productive on both sides.

What both sides agree on:

- Sustained attention is trainable.
- The training produces durable structural changes in the executive control network.
- The benefits transfer — practitioners are better at attention-demanding tasks they have never specifically practiced.
- Sustained attention is metabolically expensive and cannot be held indefinitely without rest. Even advanced practitioners need to rest the muscle.<SideNote>The metabolic-cost framing is worth dwelling on. The brain consumes about 20% of the body's resting energy budget despite being only 2% of body weight, and prefrontal cortex is among the most metabolically expensive regions. Sustained executive engagement runs through that budget. Long-term practitioners get more attention per unit of metabolic spend — but they are still spending. This explains a feature of advanced practice that beginners sometimes miss: experienced meditators take breaks, deliberately, even in long retreats. The cushion time is not unlimited, even for them.</SideNote>

These claims are well-supported, consistent across the literature, and broadly accepted in cognitive science. The disagreements are at finer grain — which specific subnetworks are most affected, which training styles produce the most transfer, what the dose-response curves look like.

## The dependence on earlier limbs

Dhāraṇā does not work without the limbs that precede it. A mind carrying the cognitive load of the [yamas](/awareness/yamas)' unfinished business — deception, harm, grasping — cannot sustain attention. A body that has not been settled by [āsana](/awareness/asana) keeps tugging attention back to itself. An autonomic state that has not been regulated by [prāṇāyāma](/awareness/pranayama) keeps producing arousal that competes for attention. Sensory input that has not been gated by [pratyāhāra](/awareness/pratyahara) keeps capturing attention against the practitioner's will.

This is why the order of the limbs is not arbitrary. Dhāraṇā is the work the entire practice has been preparing for. The earlier limbs are not separate disciplines; they are the conditions that make dhāraṇā possible. A modern practitioner who tries to start with dhāraṇā — sitting down to "meditate" with no work on the earlier limbs — is not wrong to try, but is operating on a substrate that the limb was not designed to work on. The result is often frustration: attention fails repeatedly, the practice feels impossible, the practitioner concludes that they "can't meditate" when what is actually happening is that they have skipped the substrate preparation.

## The honest summary

Dhāraṇā is sustained selective attention. Its substrate is the executive control network. Modern cognitive neuroscience has independently identified the substrate, measured the training effect in long-term practitioners, and validated the transferability of the skill across attention-demanding tasks the practitioners never specifically trained on.

This is the limb where the Eastern and Western vocabularies overlap most completely. They are, in different languages, describing the same thing, training the same circuitry, and noticing the same difficulties along the way. The convergence is not coincidence. It is two methods of inquiry finding the same feature of the nervous system from opposite sides.

The structural function of the sixth limb is to take an attentional capacity that everyone has — momentary focus — and cultivate it into a sustained capacity that few people develop without deliberate training. The trained capacity is then the substrate that the seventh limb (*dhyāna*) will operate on, in a state where the effort of holding attention has dropped out and attention simply remains.
