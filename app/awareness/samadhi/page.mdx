*Samādhi* is the eighth limb, and it is where the editorial discipline of this whole project earns its keep. There is enormous temptation, on a page like this, to inflate. There is equal temptation, on a page like this, to flatten — to reduce samādhi to whatever the imaging can currently see. Both moves betray the material. The task is to say what can honestly be said, mark what cannot, and leave the rest for the reader to sit with.

## What the Sūtras say

The classical definition at *Yoga Sūtras* 3.3: *tad eva artha-mātra-nirbhāsam svarūpa-śūnyam iva samādhiḥ* — "[dhyāna] itself, when only the object shines forth, as though emptied of its own form, is samādhi."

This is dense. Let me parse it. *Tad eva* — "that very thing" — refers back to dhyāna. *Artha-mātra-nirbhāsam* — "only the object shining forth." *Svarūpa-śūnyam iva* — "as though emptied of its own [the meditator's] form." The composite picture: dhyāna deepens into a state where the object of attention dominates awareness so completely that the practitioner's own structure as a separate subject seems to drop away. The *iva* (as though) is, again, doing careful work. The practitioner does not literally cease; the felt structure of being a separate subject becomes, briefly, not present.

Patañjali distinguishes several sub-types: *samprajñāta* (with conceptual content) and *asamprajñāta* (without conceptual content); *sabīja* (with seed) and *nirbīja* (without seed). The deeper distinctions need not concern us here.<SideNote>For readers who want to follow them, Bryant's commentary on *Sūtras* 1.17–1.51 is the cleanest exposition I know. The sub-classifications represent an extended phenomenological taxonomy of contemplative depth states, distinguished by what remains in awareness at each level — initially conceptual content, then non-conceptual content, then a state of "seedless" awareness with no specifiable content. Whether these correspond to different empirically distinguishable states is an open research question.</SideNote> The structural claim across all sub-types is the same: at the depth of contemplative practice, the apparent self does not merely quiet. It is, briefly, not there.

This is a claim about phenomenology, made by practitioners across many centuries, in many traditions, with remarkable consistency. It is not a claim about the absence of a brain. Practitioners come out of samādhi and report on it. The brain was running the whole time. What was missing, by report, was the felt structure of being someone running it.

## What neuroscience can and cannot measure

Neuroscience cannot currently measure the absence of a felt self. It can measure brain activity, and it can correlate activity with reported experience, but the experience itself remains inaccessible to direct measurement. This is not a failure of imaging technology; it is the hard problem of consciousness,<Cite id="chalmers-1995" /> which no scan can dissolve.

What neuroscience *can* measure is structural correlates of reported deep states. Three findings matter most.

First, in advanced practitioners, the default mode network — the substrate of self-referential thought — shows reduced activity during deep meditation, and the reduction extends beyond the meditation session into rest periods.<Cite id="brewer-2011" />

Second, the usual anti-correlation between the default mode network and the executive control network — the two networks that normally trade off, one being active when the other is not — is reduced or absent during reported non-dual states. Zoran Josipovic's<Cite id="josipovic-2014" /> imaging of Tibetan Buddhist practitioners during reported non-dual awareness found this signature. The networks that normally take turns are instead operating together, or perhaps neither is operating in its usual mode.

Third, the networks that produce the sense of being a self located in a body — including the medial prefrontal cortex, the temporoparietal junction, and the insula — show reduced activity in advanced practitioners during deep states. The apparatus that normally constructs a located self is, in some measurable sense, less busy.

The entropic-brain framework<Cite id="carhart-harris-2014" /> adds a useful integrating concept. The "primary states" Carhart-Harris identifies — psychedelic experience, REM dreaming, early developmental cognition, deep meditation — all share an elevated-entropy signature: more disordered neural activity, less constraint by the default mode network's organizing pull. The REBUS extension<Cite id="carhart-harris-2019" /> proposes that these states involve the relaxation of high-level priors that ordinarily impose interpretive structure on experience. Samādhi, on this reading, may be a primary state in Carhart-Harris's sense — and the structural overlap with high-dose psychedelic experiences in advanced practitioners is striking, if not yet rigorously characterized.<SideNote>The overlap between deep contemplative states and psychedelic states is real, well-documented, and often misunderstood. The phenomenology overlaps significantly: ego dissolution, unity experiences, time distortion, ineffability, a sense of having contacted something more fundamental than ordinary experience. The mechanisms appear to involve similar networks (DMN quieting, network re-coupling). The differences also matter: psychedelics typically do not produce durable changes in baseline function in the way that meditation does, and meditation does not produce the specific perceptual changes (visuals, synesthesia) characteristic of psychedelic states. Both are doing related things; neither is the other.</SideNote>

## Network dissolution, not network activation

Putting the findings together: samādhi appears to be characterized not by the activation of any particular region or network but by the *quieting* and *re-coupling* of the networks that normally produce the located self. The apparatus dissolves. Whatever remains is not located in any scan we can currently take.

This is the most defensible neuroscientific claim about samādhi that the current literature supports. It is also the claim that most cleanly matches the phenomenological reports across contemplative traditions. The match is not proof — it is consistency, which is much weaker — but consistency between two completely independent methods of inquiry is worth noticing.

The structural fact that grounds this defensibility: there is no region of the brain that is "you." The sense of self is constructed, in real time, by the coordinated activity of multiple networks. Damage one part and a specific feature of the self goes — the body-ownership signal, the autobiographical memory, the moral agency. None of these is the self; all of them together make up what we call one. Samādhi is, on this reading, the temporary suspension of that construction. The networks that normally compose the self are, for the duration of the state, not composing it.

## The visual argument

The 3D atlas in the [explore view](/explore) of this site lets you light up regions one at a time. Each of the earlier limbs in this wing points to specific anatomy: [insula](/regions/insula) for interoception, [thalamus](/regions/thalamus) for sensory gating, prefrontal regions for sustained attention. The map accumulates as the limbs progress.

Samādhi does not point to a region. It points to the dissolution of the apparatus the earlier regions form. If you toggle the [awareness mode](/explore) and click samādhi, the regions already highlighted fade. The map dissolves.

This is not a stylistic flourish. It is the structural argument of the whole framework, made visually. The earlier limbs train specific networks; samādhi is what is left when those networks no longer produce their usual constructions. The map is not the territory, and at the eighth limb the map and the territory come apart in the most explicit possible way.

## The hard problem, briefly and honestly

The hard problem of consciousness<Cite id="chalmers-1995" /><Cite id="nagel-1974" /> — why there is something it is like to be a brain at all — is not solved by anything in this essay. The eight limbs describe what awareness *does* when it is trained. They do not explain what awareness *is*. Samādhi, on a strict reading, is a state in which the usual constructions of self quiet enough that whatever lies beneath them becomes more nakedly available. What lies beneath them is the hard problem.

Different traditions name what remains differently — *sākṣī* in Advaita, the pure recognition of *pratyabhijñā* in Kashmir Shaivism, *puruṣa* in Sāṃkhya, the *original mind* in Zen, *rigpa* in Dzogchen, *fanāʾ* in Sufism. The naming is theology; the pointing-at is phenomenology; the existence of the thing pointed at is a philosophical claim no scan will settle.

Metzinger's<Cite id="metzinger-2003" /> deflationary move is one option: there is no self that needs to be explained; the self is a model the brain runs, and samādhi is the model temporarily relaxing. The hard problem then becomes a problem about why the model is accompanied by phenomenal experience at all, which Metzinger does not solve but reframes. The contemplative traditions take a different option: they treat what remains in samādhi as something *more fundamental* than the self-construction, something the practice has recognized rather than constructed. Whether this recognition tracks something real or is itself a refined construction is the deepest question the framework cannot answer from inside itself.

## What does and does not follow

Three things follow from the structural reading that are worth being explicit about.

One: samādhi is real in the sense that the structural correlates of practitioners' reports are measurable. The DMN-quieting is replicated; the network-coupling shifts are replicated; the phenomenology is consistent across traditions in suggestive ways. The state is not an illusion to be debunked.

Two: samādhi as described in the *Sūtras* is *more* than the structural correlates we can measure. The reports include phenomenal qualities — recognition, clarity, a felt rightness — that imaging cannot capture. Whether those phenomenal qualities are constructed by the brain in the moment, or whether they reveal something the brain ordinarily occludes, is exactly the philosophical question the hard problem names. Neither neuroscience nor contemplative tradition is in a position to settle it from its own resources alone.

Three: the structural reading does not require taking samādhi any less seriously than the traditional reading does. The eighth limb describes a state of contemplative depth that practitioners have spent decades cultivating. The state is rare, demanding, and almost certainly underdescribed by both the traditional metaphysical framing and the modern reductive framing. The most honest position is to take the practitioners' reports seriously as data, take the imaging seriously as data, and notice the convergence between them without forcing either side to capitulate.

## What can and cannot be said

Samādhi, as described by Patañjali and the broader contemplative tradition, is the dissolution of the subject-object structure of experience. Neuroscience cannot measure this directly. It can measure the structural correlates — the quieting of networks that normally produce the located self, the altered coupling between networks that normally trade off, the entropic signature shared with other primary states. The correlates are consistent with the phenomenological reports. The reports are consistent across traditions. The convergence is real and unexplained.

The eighth limb is what the practice is for. It is also, in a precise sense, what the practice is *not* — the moment at which the practitioner is no longer the practitioner and the practice is no longer the practice. The map dissolves. Whatever remains is not what this essay is about. It is what the essay has been pointing at, all along, from a distance the essay cannot close.
