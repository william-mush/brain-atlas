*Dhyāna* is the seventh limb, and it is most usefully defined by its difference from the sixth. Patañjali at *Yoga Sūtras* 3.2: *tatra pratyaya-ekatānatā dhyānam* — "the uninterrupted flow of consciousness toward that [object of dhāraṇā] is dhyāna." [Dhāraṇā](/awareness/dharana) is the muscle of attention working. Dhyāna is what happens when the muscle stops needing to work — when attention is no longer held by effort but simply remains where it is.

The *Sūtras* describe dhyāna as an unbroken flow of awareness toward the object, and they note that the distinction between the meditator and the meditated-upon begins to thin. This is not yet samādhi — there is still a meditator — but the subject-object structure that ordinary experience takes for granted is starting to loosen.

## The neural signature

The shift from dhāraṇā to dhyāna has a measurable neural correlate, and it is one of the more striking findings in the contemplative neuroscience literature.

Brewer and colleagues' 2011 fMRI work<Cite id="brewer-2011" /> on experienced meditators showed reduced activity in the default mode network — particularly the posterior cingulate cortex and the medial prefrontal cortex — during meditation. The reduction was sustained even at rest, suggesting durable changes in the network's baseline activity rather than just transient state-effects during the meditation session. The default mode network is the substrate of self-referential thought, mind-wandering, autobiographical memory, and the constant background construction of a felt self. When it quiets, the felt self quiets too.

At the same time, the executive control network signature that characterizes dhāraṇā — heavy DLPFC engagement, the felt effort of holding attention — decreases. Attention is still on the object, but the effort of holding it has dropped. This is the precise neural signature of the experiential shift the *Sūtras* describe.<SideNote>Brewer's group has since extended this work in several useful directions, including showing that the DMN-quieting effect correlates with self-reported reduction in mind-wandering, that it persists in experienced practitioners even when they are not meditating, and that the specific PCC region most affected has been further localized in higher-resolution studies. The 2011 paper is the foundational result; the follow-up literature has filled in details that the original could only sketch.</SideNote>

## What "resting in" means, mechanistically

In dhāraṇā, the practitioner repeatedly notices that attention has wandered and brings it back. The cognitive load is real: every return is a small executive operation. Each return engages DLPFC, costs metabolic energy, takes a moment of conscious effort.

In dhyāna, the returns become unnecessary because the wandering has largely stopped. The default mode network — the source of the wandering — has quieted enough that attention does not have to defend itself constantly. The system has shifted into a state where the natural drift toward self-referential thought is no longer happening with anything like its ordinary frequency.

This is not a mystical state. It is a measurable shift in network balance. The network that normally produces self-referential drift is less active. The network that holds the object remains active but no longer needs to suppress the drift. Attention persists, with less work.

The predictive-processing account adds something. On Friston's active-inference framework,<Cite id="friston-2010" /> sustained attention to an object is the maintenance of a high-precision prior about what should be attended to. In dhāraṇā, this prior is being actively held against competing priors (about the self, about the future, about ongoing concerns). In dhyāna, the competing priors have relaxed enough that the attention prior persists without active maintenance. The model has reached an attractor state where the desired attention is the default rather than the imposed condition.<SideNote>This is, again, a translation between vocabularies more than a tested finding. The Friston framework is sufficiently flexible to accommodate descriptions of many cognitive states; whether it predicts the specific signature of dhyāna in advance, or whether it merely accommodates the signature after the fact, is a fair criticism that applies to predictive processing's relationship to contemplative experience more broadly. The framework's strength is its generality; its weakness is the same generality.</SideNote>

## The thinning of the subject-object boundary

The classical description of dhyāna says the boundary between the meditator and the meditated-upon thins. This sounds mystical and is, in fact, a structural claim that current neuroscience can partially address.

The sense of being a subject — a someone, located somewhere, looking at something — is constructed by networks that include the medial prefrontal cortex, the posterior cingulate, the temporoparietal junction, and the insula. None of these is "the self"; together they produce the felt structure of being one. When these networks quiet, what they normally produce also quiets. The practitioner does not stop existing, but the felt sense of being someone observing the breath becomes less pronounced.

This is, I should emphasize, the structural claim and not a metaphysical one. The practitioner reports a thinning of the boundary; the imaging shows quieting of the boundary-construction networks. Whether this means the boundary was *never real* (Advaita), *never had inherent existence* (Madhyamaka), *was a recognition-event* (Shaivism), or *is a constructed model the brain runs* (Metzinger) is the philosophical question the [Advaita & Shaivism page](/awareness/advaita-shaivism) and the [theories page](/awareness/theories) take up. The structural fact — that the construction is happening, and that contemplative practice modulates it — is what the imaging supports.

## Dhāraṇā and dhyāna are coupled

Patañjali treats the two limbs as a pair. They are not separate practices; they are phases of the same practice. The practitioner begins with dhāraṇā — effortful selective attention — and the practice ripens into dhyāna when the effort is no longer required.

The neuroscience supports this coupling. The transition is not binary; it is a gradient. Skilled practitioners can move fluidly between the two modes, increasing effort when attention flags and releasing it when attention is stable. The brain signature shifts accordingly. The two networks — executive control and default mode — are in a constant negotiation, and sustained practice changes the terms of the negotiation.

This is also a point where the focused-attention / open-monitoring distinction<Cite id="lutz-slagter-2008" /> becomes useful. The Lutz framework treats FA and OM as distinct training arcs. Patañjali's text suggests that they are continuous: dhāraṇā is the FA phase, dhyāna ripens into something closer to OM, and the practitioner is meant to develop both. Modern practitioners often emphasize one or the other depending on their lineage (concentration-emphasis vs. insight-emphasis traditions), but the *Sūtras* are integrated about it. Both are needed; both prepare the eighth limb.

## What dhyāna is and is not

A small clarification because this limb is often misread. Dhyāna is not "deep meditation" in the loose sense the word often gets used in. It is a specific state with a specific signature: sustained attention without effort, default mode quieted, self-referential drift suspended. It is not absence of thought (thoughts can arise and pass), not loss of consciousness (the practitioner is awake), not absorption-without-content (there is still an object of attention).

It is also, to be honest, not common. The state Patañjali is describing is a state that experienced practitioners report after substantial training. The casual meditator who closes their eyes for twenty minutes a day is unlikely to be in dhyāna most of the time; they are doing the earlier work that may, over time, develop into it. This is not a criticism of casual practice. It is a recognition that the limb refers to something specific, not to any meditation-shaped activity.

The cognitive-neuroscience evidence is consistent with this. The dramatic DMN-quieting Brewer documented is most reliable in practitioners with thousands of hours of accumulated practice. Beginners' meditation shows much smaller effects, and the effects in eight-week mindfulness courses, while real, are modest relative to what long-term practitioners show.<Cite id="van-dam-2018" /> The state is trainable; the training is real work.

## Stocktaking

Dhyāna is the state in which sustained attention persists without the effort that initially established it. It is characterized by reduced default mode activity, reduced executive effort signature, and a felt thinning of the subject-object distinction. The neural correlates are consistent across studies; the experiential description is consistent across centuries. The seventh limb is not the eighth — there is still a meditator — but it is the structural precondition for what the eighth describes.

The two limbs together — dhāraṇā and dhyāna — describe the work of attention training as a developmental arc. The arc has been independently mapped by Western cognitive science under different names. The convergence is not perfect, but it is striking enough that the burden of proof has shifted: the question is no longer whether contemplative practice produces measurable effects on attention. It does. The questions are what those effects are, how they relate to the experiential reports practitioners give, and whether they generalize across populations less studied than the small samples we have. The work continues.
