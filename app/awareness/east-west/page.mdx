A risk in any extended treatment of one contemplative system is that the system starts to sound like the only one that exists, or like the one all the others are unsuccessfully approximating. Patañjali has had two and a half millennia of careful commentary; he is also one tradition among many, and many of the others did not have access to his framework and still produced descriptions of contemplative experience that overlap with his in startling ways.

This page is the corrective. Each of the traditions below developed independently, or in partial dialogue with the others, and each produced a phenomenology of trained awareness that is *structurally similar* to what Patañjali describes — but with different vocabulary, different metaphysics, and different ideas about what the practice is *for*. Reading them next to each other does two useful things. It shows what is robust across traditions and what is culturally specific. It also disciplines the impulse to read any single tradition's metaphysics as the literal description of what is happening.

## Why this page exists

A small methodological note before we start. The structural similarity of contemplative reports across traditions is sometimes taken as evidence for what philosophers of religion call the *perennialist* thesis — that there is a single ultimate reality which contemplatives in different cultures are independently accessing.<SideNote>Aldous Huxley\'s *The Perennial Philosophy* (1945) is the popular statement; the academic version goes back to William James\' *Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) and forward through Walter Stace, Robert Forman, and others. The thesis has been pushed back on hard by Steven Katz and the so-called "constructivist" school, who argue that mystical experiences are shaped by the conceptual and ritual framework the mystic brings to them, and that the apparent cross-cultural convergence is an artifact of selective reading. Both positions overstate. The genuine question is which features of contemplative experience are universal (or near-universal) features of trained nervous systems and which are culturally shaped — and that is, in principle, empirically tractable.</SideNote> The perennialist thesis is more contestable than it sounds. The other reading — and the one I will lean on here — is that the structural similarity reflects something *about the nervous systems doing the contemplating*, not about an ultimate reality being accessed. If trained human awareness has stable structural features, then independent traditions cultivating that awareness should arrive at similar reports for reasons that have to do with the substrate, not the metaphysics.

The reports below are taken seriously on their own terms first. The structural reading comes at the end.

## Theravāda Abhidhamma: the 89 cittas

The Theravāda tradition, preserved primarily in Sri Lanka, Burma, and Thailand, developed what is probably the most granular phenomenological taxonomy of mind ever produced. The system is laid out in Buddhaghosa's 5th-century *Visuddhimagga*<Cite id="buddhaghosa-visuddhimagga" /> and the Abhidhamma Piṭaka texts it commentaries on, and it has been refined for fifteen centuries.

The central unit is the *citta* — a moment of consciousness, characterized by what it is conscious of (its *ārammaṇa*, object) and by the mental factors (*cetasikā*) accompanying it. The Theravāda enumerates 89 distinct cittas, classified along several dimensions: *kusala* (wholesome) vs *akusala* (unwholesome) vs *abyākata* (indeterminate), the realm of existence the citta operates in (sensual / fine-material / immaterial / supramundane), and the specific configuration of mental factors present.<SideNote>The full enumeration is 121 in the expanded analysis that includes the supramundane states; 89 in the standard reckoning. The system parses experience much finer than Patañjali does — where the Yoga Sūtras give a handful of mental modifications (*vṛtti*) and call it good, the Abhidhamma gives 52 mental factors and 89 (or 121) ways they combine. Whether this finer-grained taxonomy is empirically vindicated or just exhaustingly thorough is a fair question.</SideNote>

What this taxonomy lets the Theravāda practitioner do is *recognize* their current state. A trained meditator can, in principle, identify which of the 89 cittas is occurring at a given moment — which is what *vipassanā*, insight meditation, cultivates. Modern Burmese teachers in the Mahasi lineage describe noting practice as the systematic application of Abhidhamma categories to ongoing experience.

For Western cognitive neuroscience, the Abhidhamma offers something specific: a phenomenological data structure refined over centuries by introspective expertise. If you wanted to design a study that mapped neural correlates onto fine-grained mental states, you could do worse than starting with the Abhidhamma classification scheme. Whether the categories carve nature at its joints — whether the 89 cittas correspond to anything the imaging can detect — is an empirical question that has not, to my knowledge, been seriously attempted.<SideNote>"Has not been seriously attempted" is doing real work. There are studies that use *vipassanā*-style noting practice as the meditation condition; there are studies that compare specific Theravāda states (such as the *jhānas*) to other meditation styles. But a systematic project to test whether Abhidhamma categories predict distinguishable neural signatures is not in the literature I have read. It would be a significant undertaking — the Abhidhamma is not a list of 89 things one could just dump into an fMRI analysis without a long phenomenological-validation process first — but it is the kind of project a serious contemplative neuroscience program would eventually need to do.</SideNote>

## Madhyamaka: emptiness and the rejection of essence

If Patañjali takes for granted that contemplative practice has objects (breath, mantra, image), Madhyamaka asks a harder question: do those objects exist in the way we ordinarily take them to? Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*<Cite id="nagarjuna-mmk" /><Cite id="garfield-1995" /> (~200 CE) argues that nothing — phenomena, persons, mental states, the practice itself — has *svabhāva* (inherent existence). Everything is empty (*śūnya*) in the precise sense of lacking an independent essence.

This is not nihilism, which Nāgārjuna explicitly rejects. The point is not that things do not exist; the point is that things exist *only* in dependence on other things, on conceptual designations, on conventions. Madhyamaka distinguishes two truths: conventional truth (the level at which we say "the cup is on the table") and ultimate truth (the level at which "cup," "table," and the relations between them are recognized as conventional designations rather than ultimate descriptions).

The relation to modern cognitive science is more striking than it has any right to be. Predictive processing, in the Friston-Clark-Seth form, holds that perceived objects are *models* — constructions the brain generates rather than passive registrations of mind-independent things. The objects we perceive lack inherent existence in a precise sense: they are produced by the modeling process; they are emptied, on careful inspection, of the standalone independence we naively attribute to them.<SideNote>Jay Garfield and Evan Thompson, among others, have made versions of this comparison directly. The convergence is suggestive but should not be overstated. Nāgārjuna was making a metaphysical argument about the nature of phenomena in general; Friston is describing a computational architecture in brains specifically. The argument forms are different. The conclusions about the constructed nature of perceived objects are similar enough to be worth noticing.</SideNote>

For contemplative practice, Madhyamaka has a distinctive role. It is not so much another meditation system as a *philosophical orientation* that other meditation systems can adopt. A Tibetan Buddhist practitioner working in any tantric framework will typically have studied Madhyamaka first, because the metaphysical position shapes what the practice is taken to be doing. You are not, on this account, encountering ultimate reality. You are recognizing that the apparent self and the apparent objects of awareness are conventionally real but ultimately empty.

## Yogācāra: mind-only

The other major Indian Buddhist school, Yogācāra (also called Vijñānavāda or Cittamātra), developed mainly through Asaṅga and Vasubandhu in the 4th century CE. Where Madhyamaka emphasizes emptiness, Yogācāra emphasizes the constructive activity of consciousness itself.<Cite id="vasubandhu-twenty" /><Cite id="siderits-yogacara" />

The central claim is that what we perceive are *cognitions* — mental representations — rather than mind-independent objects. Vasubandhu's *Viṃśatikā* (Twenty Verses) defends this through a comparative analysis: dreams, illusions, and shared hallucinations all exhibit the structural features of ordinary perception (clarity, spatial coherence, the sense of meeting an independent world) without requiring external objects. If we cannot tell from the inside whether a perception has an external referent, perhaps the supposed external referents are doing no philosophical work.<SideNote>This is, of course, structurally similar to Berkeley\'s idealism (1710) and to various contemporary internalist positions in philosophy of mind. The Yogācāra arguments are older by twelve centuries and, for what it's worth, more careful than Berkeley's — they do not require the deus ex machina of God's perception to keep the world stable across observers, because the Yogācāra account has the ālaya-vijñāna doing that work.</SideNote>

Yogācāra introduces a structure that has no clean parallel in Patañjali: the *ālaya-vijñāna* or "storehouse consciousness." This is an underlying stream of consciousness that carries karmic seeds (*bīja*) from moment to moment and provides the continuity of experience that ordinary consciousness lacks. It is what persists in deep meditative absorption when surface awareness ceases. It is also what flows from life to life on the karmic accounts that Yogācāra takes seriously.

For modern cognitive science, the ālaya-vijñāna is interesting because it names a layer of cognitive function that ordinary introspective access cannot reach but that nonetheless shapes ordinary experience. The implicit-memory systems studied in cognitive psychology — procedural memory, conditioning, the influences on judgment that subjects cannot report — sit in something like the explanatory role Yogācāra assigned to the storehouse. The metaphysical commitments differ; the structural function in the model is similar.

## Dzogchen: rigpa, the recognition of awareness

The Tibetan tradition's most rigorous attempt to describe deep contemplative experience from the inside. Dzogchen ("Great Perfection") is the highest of the nine vehicles in the Nyingma school of Tibetan Buddhism, and its central concept is *rigpa* — pure awareness, the recognized basis.<Cite id="longchenpa" />

Dzogchen sharply distinguishes *rigpa* from *sems* (ordinary mind, *citta* in Sanskrit). Ordinary mind is dualistic: there is an experiencer and there are experiences. Rigpa is the recognition of awareness as the open, knowing background within which both experiencer and experiences arise — the recognition that whatever appears, appears *to* awareness, and that awareness itself is not another object that appears.

The practices have two main families. *Trekchö* ("cutting through") is the practice of recognizing rigpa directly, in the midst of ordinary activity, by cutting through the conceptual elaboration that ordinarily obscures it. *Tögal* ("crossing over") involves specific gazes and postures that produce visionary experiences taken to be expressions of awareness's natural luminosity.

The structural parallel with Kashmir Shaivite *pratyabhijñā* (recognition) is striking and probably not accidental — there was philosophical contact between Indian and Tibetan tantric traditions in the first millennium CE. Both traditions describe contemplative awakening as the recognition of something already present rather than the achievement of something new. The Tibetan vocabulary is more developed; the practices are more systematized.

For modern neuroscience, Dzogchen presents a specific challenge. The state it describes — pure awareness recognizing itself, undistracted by content but not unconscious — is exactly the kind of state that does not have a clean neural correlate. It is not a relaxed state (the practitioner is alert), not a focused state (there is no specific object), not a default-mode state (there is no narrative self-referential drift). Josipovic's<Cite id="josipovic-2014" /> imaging work on Tibetan practitioners during reported non-dual states suggests altered network coupling — reduced anti-correlation between default mode and executive networks — but the sample sizes are small and the interpretation is contested.

## Daoist contemplative practice: zuòwàng and wúwéi

The Chinese tradition's contemplative material is more diffuse than the Indian or Tibetan corpora — the Daoist canon is enormous and not organized for easy study — but the central practice is identifiable. *Zuòwàng* (坐忘, "sitting and forgetting") appears first in Chapter 6 of the *Zhuāngzǐ*<Cite id="zhuangzi" /> in a famous dialogue:

> Yán Huí said: "I am making progress." Confucius said: "What do you mean?" Yán Huí said: "I have forgotten benevolence and righteousness." [...] On another day Yán Huí said: "I am making progress." Confucius said: "What do you mean?" Yán Huí said: "I sit and forget." Confucius, startled, said: "What do you mean, sit and forget?" Yán Huí said: "I slough off my limbs and trunk, dim my intelligence, depart from my form, leave knowledge behind, and become identical with the Transformational Thoroughfare. This is what I mean by sitting and forgetting."

The practice was formalized in Sīmǎ Chéngzhēn's 8th-century *Zuòwànglùn*<Cite id="kohn-2010" />, which lays out a seven-stage developmental progression from respect-and-faith through karma-interception through detachment to realization of the Dao. The system is not identical to Patañjali's — there is no equivalent of prāṇāyāma as a distinct limb, no yamas-niyamas list, no samādhi terminology — but the structural arc is parallel: ethical preparation, body settling, mind settling, deep absorption, recognition.

*Wúwéi* (無為, "non-action" or "effortless action") is the orientation that the practice cultivates and that the *Dào Dé Jīng* names as the central virtue. Not literal inaction — that would be impossible and pointless. The cultivated capacity to act *without* the forcing that grasping and aversion introduce. Acting from a settled and responsive substrate rather than a noisy one. The relationship to Patañjali's later limbs is clear: a practitioner in *dhyāna* would, in Daoist terms, be acting from *wúwéi*.

## Sufi material: fanāʾ and baqāʾ

The Islamic mystical tradition has its own contemplative phenomenology, developed primarily through the Sufi orders from the 8th century onward. The central concept is *fanāʾ* — annihilation, the extinction of the self in God — paired with *baqāʾ*, the subsisting of the self in God after annihilation.<SideNote>I am treating this section more briefly than the others for honest reasons: my reading in the Sufi tradition is shallower than my reading in the Indian and East Asian material. I am including it because to omit it would falsely suggest the convergence is an Asian-only phenomenon. Ibn ʿArabī, Rūmī, al-Ghazālī, and the broader Sufi corpus reward serious engagement that this essay does not give them. A future revision should expand this section with help from someone who knows the material better than I do.</SideNote>

The structural parallel with samādhi is, for the third time on this page, striking. Fanāʾ is described in the Sufi literature as the dissolution of the subject — the practitioner's individual identity drops away, leaving (in the theistic vocabulary) only God. Baqāʾ is the return to ordinary functioning, but with the recognition that the apparent self is, and was, a transient configuration of awareness rather than a substantial entity.

The theological framing is, of course, completely different from the Buddhist. The Sufi practitioner is not realizing emptiness; they are being absorbed into a personal God. Whether the *experience* underlying these different descriptions is structurally similar — or whether the apparent similarity is an artifact of selective reading by Western comparative scholars — is exactly the question this page is trying not to settle prematurely.

## The structural argument, carefully

What do we make of the convergence?

The strong perennialist claim — that all these traditions are accessing the same ultimate reality, just describing it through different cultural lenses — is more than the evidence supports. The descriptions converge in some places (the dissolution of subject-object structure, the cultivability of awareness, the apparent universality of certain meditative milestones) and diverge in others (whether what is dissolved is illusion or recognition, whether the goal is liberation from a personal God or absorption into one, whether the awareness left behind is empty or full).

The strong constructivist claim — that the descriptions only seem similar because comparative scholars cherry-pick — is also more than the evidence supports. The convergence is too specific in too many places to be entirely an artifact of selection. The Theravāda descriptions of the *jhānas* and the Tibetan descriptions of *śamatha* states differ at a fine grain but agree at the coarse grain on what changes as concentration deepens. Independent traditions, with no doctrinal communication, arriving at the same coarse-grained structural features is at minimum suggestive of something more than chance.

The middle position, and the one this page leans on: the convergence reflects something stable about trained human awareness. Independent traditions cultivating awareness with similar (though not identical) techniques arrive at similar (though not identical) reports because the substrate is the same. The metaphysical interpretations vary because the metaphysical interpretations are downstream of the experience, not the cause of it.

This is, frankly, also where modern contemplative neuroscience comes in.<SideNote>B. Alan Wallace's *Contemplative Science*<Cite id="wallace-2007" /> is the most explicit argument for treating contemplative traditions as a source of expertise that neuroscience needs. Wallace spent fourteen years as a Tibetan Buddhist monk before training as a Stanford philosopher of science; he is unusually well-placed to make the case for the dialogue this section is gesturing at.</SideNote> If the convergence is structural — if it reflects features of trained nervous systems rather than features of an underlying reality being accessed — then imaging should eventually be able to identify the structural correlates. We are not there yet. The studies that exist are small, the populations are unrepresentative, the controls are weak. But the project is at least coherent, in a way that the perennialist project (which is metaphysical) and the strong constructivist project (which is sociological) are not.

The eight-limb framework, taken structurally, is one map of this substrate. The Theravāda, Madhyamaka, Yogācāra, Dzogchen, Daoist, and Sufi traditions provide other maps, with different emphases and different blind spots. The maps disagree about the territory's metaphysics. They agree, in striking and underappreciated ways, about the territory's shape.
