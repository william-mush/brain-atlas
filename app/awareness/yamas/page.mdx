The first limb of Patañjali's eightfold path is the *yamas*: five things to refrain from. The traditional list, in the order Patañjali gives at Yoga Sūtras 2.30, is *ahiṃsā* (non-harming), *satya* (truthfulness), *asteya* (non-stealing), *brahmacarya* (sensory restraint), and *aparigraha* (non-grasping).<SideNote>The Sanskrit terms are surprisingly stable across translations and traditions, but the English glosses vary in ways that matter. Brahmacarya in particular is sometimes translated as "celibacy," which captures its narrowest historical reading; "sensory restraint" or "conservation of vital energy" capture broader readings that the *Sūtras* themselves support. I am using the broader reading throughout because the narrower one cuts the limb off from most modern practitioners' lives in ways that do not seem to be in the text's intent.</SideNote>

The standard reading treats these as a moral code — five rules to live by, presented at the head of the contemplative manual as ethical prerequisites for what follows. This is not wrong, and it is not the whole of what is being said. The structural reading, which this page leans on, is that the yamas name five specific *cognitive loads* the nervous system pays for when they are present, and the contemplative work the later limbs ask for cannot be done on a substrate that is carrying those loads.

That structural reading is mine, and I want to be explicit about it. Patañjali says "do not harm." I am saying "harm produces threat-monitoring overhead that the substrate cannot afford." These are not the same claim. The structural reading is consistent with the text and explains why the limb is *first* (rather than tenth, or absent), but it is an interpretation, not a translation. Read accordingly.

The five yamas, in order.

## Ahiṃsā — non-harming

The most famous yama, the one Gandhi popularized, the one most readers come to the list knowing about. Its scope in the *Sūtras* is broad: not just physical harm but the broader category of harmful action through body, speech, and mind. The traditional commentaries draw out specific applications — restraint from killing, from wounding, from cruel speech, from carrying contempt, from holding grudges that produce harmful action downstream.

The structural cost of harm is sustained vigilance. If you have harmed someone, three things follow that the nervous system pays for. The harmed party may retaliate, so the threat-monitoring system stays partially active. The action may be discovered, so attention has to be diverted to managing concealment or rationalization. The harm may need to be re-inflicted, if the original purpose remains unmet, which means the system is partially committed to a future repetition rather than fully available to the present.

None of this requires the harm-doer to think about the harm consciously. The autonomic costs are paid below the threshold of report. The sympathetic system runs slightly hot. Sleep is slightly less consolidated. Attention is slightly more fragmented.<SideNote>I am being a little casual here in attributing these costs specifically to harm-doing rather than to the more general category of unresolved interpersonal conflict. The research literature on sustained threat-monitoring and its autonomic costs is robust; the specific isolation of harm-doing as a separable variable is less so. Read this paragraph as plausibility argument rather than as a direct citation of controlled studies.</SideNote>

The Sanskrit is precise. *Ahiṃsā* is not "kindness" — it is the negation of *hiṃsā*, the absence of harm. The yama prescribes a floor, not a ceiling. You are not required to be benevolent; you are required to not be actively harmful. The minimum is doable in a way the maximum would not be, and the minimum is what the substrate's noise floor needs.

The neuroscience adjacent to this claim is the broader literature on chronic threat and its costs. Sustained stress responses produce measurable changes in hippocampal volume, in HPA-axis regulation, in inflammatory markers. The contemplative-neuroscience literature has not, to my knowledge, isolated harm-doing-as-such as the variable; it has worked instead with the larger category of stress and the smaller category of mindfulness. But the structural argument is the same: a substrate paying chronic autonomic costs is not the substrate the later limbs need.

## Satya — truthfulness

The cleanest case for the structural reading. Lying is *expensive*. The deception-imaging literature, while contested in its applications, is consistent on the basic finding: fMRI studies of deception consistently show increased prefrontal recruitment, beyond what truthful responses require. The anterior cingulate, dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, and ventrolateral prefrontal cortex are all reliably more active during lying than during truth-telling.<SideNote>The applications of this finding to real-world lie detection have been overhyped and the underlying mechanism is debated — is the PFC activity reflecting the cognitive cost of maintaining two parallel models, the executive cost of suppressing the automatic truthful response, or something else? For our purposes the relevant fact is the cost, not its precise decomposition. Lying is harder than telling the truth, and the brain pays for that difficulty in additional engagement.</SideNote> Reaction times for lies are reliably longer than for truths.

Sustained deception over weeks or months produces what could reasonably be called executive-function depletion. The liar is maintaining, at minimum, two parallel models of reality: the model they have shared and the model they actually hold. They must adjudicate in real time, for each interlocutor and each context, which model to project. They must keep the models from contaminating each other. They must remember what they have said, to whom, when — a working-memory load that scales with the size of the lie.

Patañjali notices, by introspection across many practitioners, that a mind doing this work cannot settle into one-pointed attention. The prescription is not "lying is bad." The prescription is "lying is expensive, and the practice cannot afford the cost." *Satya* names the load. The contemplative training that follows takes for granted that the load has been lifted.

There is a subtler version of the same point. *Satya* also includes the cultivation of an internal honesty — not just truthfulness in speech but truthfulness in self-report. Many practitioners discover, when they begin contemplative work, that they have been lying to themselves about their motivations, their attachments, their reactivity. The internal lies impose the same cost as the external ones. *Svādhyāya* — self-study, the fourth niyama — works on this directly.

## Asteya — non-stealing

The yama most often dismissed as obvious, and most often misunderstood. The traditional reading extends well beyond literal theft. *Asteya* prescribes restraint from taking what is not freely given, in any form — material goods, but also credit for others' work, time others have not consented to give, attention others have not offered, ideas presented as one's own.

The cost of stealing has two components. One is the same as harm: the stolen-from party may retaliate, the action may be discovered, concealment must be maintained. The other is more interesting and more specific to this yama: stealing is downstream of a particular cognitive state, which is the conviction that what one has is not enough.

That conviction is itself a load. A mind that experiences the world as adversarial-distributional — as a fixed pie of which one must claim one's share, by whatever means — runs on sympathetic arousal as a baseline. Acquisition is the goal-state; lack is the alarm. The autonomic substrate does not get to rest. The later limbs cannot work on a substrate that does not rest.

*Asteya* prescribes restraint from the action because the action is symptomatic of the state, and the action also *reinforces* the state. Each successful theft confirms that the world is the kind of place where one must take. Each restraint from theft creates a moment in which the state can be examined and possibly relaxed. The yama is not just rule-following; it is also a deliberate refusal to feed the underlying disposition.

## Brahmacarya — restraint of the senses

The yama whose reading has shifted most across history. In the classical period, *brahmacarya* often meant celibacy specifically, and the *Sūtras* are addressed in part to a renunciate audience for whom celibacy was a practical matter. In the modern period, with the audience being largely householders, the broader reading — restraint of the senses, conservation of vital energy, avoidance of indulgences that scatter attention — is closer to what the yama is structurally doing.

The cost of sensory indulgence is the same cost that *aparigraha* names from the other side: chronic activation of the reward system. The ventral striatum, the dopaminergic projections from the ventral tegmental area, the orbitofrontal cortex tracking expected value — all of these are designed to produce pursuit behavior in response to anticipated reward.<SideNote>The addiction research literature has worked this out in extreme detail. The reward-prediction-error account of dopaminergic signaling, developed by Wolfram Schultz and others starting in the 1990s, gives a precise mechanism: dopamine neurons fire to *unexpected* rewards (or cues predicting rewards), and the firing pattern shifts as the reward becomes predictable. Sustained engagement with reinforcing stimuli — whatever they are — recruits this system continuously. Long-term recruitment produces downregulation of receptor density, increased tolerance, and the characteristic loss of pleasure-without-pursuit that addiction researchers call anhedonia.</SideNote> When activated, the system is exerting top-down pull on attention: get more, find more, anticipate more.

A practitioner whose attention is constantly being pulled by the reward system cannot easily turn that attention inward. Pratyāhāra, the fifth limb, asks for the cultivated reduction of sensory pull. Brahmacarya is the upstream condition that makes pratyāhāra possible.

The yama is not asking for joylessness. It is asking for the practitioner to not be *governed* by the pursuit of sensory pleasure — to be able to experience pleasure without immediately needing to recreate it, to be able to refuse a craving without the refusal feeling like deprivation. Modern translations sometimes render this as "moderation," which captures the right tone for most contemporary readers even if it loses some of the precision of the Sanskrit.

## Aparigraha — non-grasping

The yama most easily underestimated in cultures that have made acquisition a moral virtue. *Aparigraha* prescribes restraint from grasping — not just the action of grabbing but the underlying disposition of wanting-to-keep, wanting-to-add, wanting-to-secure-against-loss.

The cost here is the cleanest of any yama on this page. Sustained craving produces sustained activity in the ventral striatum and persistent demands on prefrontal control. The brain is, in a meaningful sense, *not at rest*. It is in a state of low-grade pursuit, even when the practitioner is sitting still and would describe themselves as at rest. The addiction-research literature has worked out the mechanism in detail. The relevant point here is that the mechanism is general — it applies to grasping after wealth, after status, after approval, after experiences, after spiritual attainments. The neural substrate does not distinguish between objects.

The contemplative implication is direct. A practitioner who is grasping — at anything — has a substrate that is partially committed to a future state different from the present one. Whatever the later limbs require of the present moment, that practitioner is partially elsewhere. *Aparigraha* prescribes the refusal to feed the disposition. Each refused grasp is, again, both a behavioral act and a refusal to reinforce the underlying state.

Patañjali offers a striking promise about this yama at *Yoga Sūtras* 2.39: when *aparigraha* is firmly established, "knowledge of the why and how of past and future births arises." This is one of the *Sūtras*' more theologically loaded predictions and is often quoted out of context. The structural reading, less mystically: when the grasping disposition is genuinely loosened, the practitioner can see, retrospectively, how earlier states were shaped by the grasping they were unaware of. The "past births" are, on a charitable structural reading, the prior states of one's own mind.<SideNote>I am, here, doing what Edwin Bryant calls "demythologizing" the *Sūtras* — taking claims that are explicitly metaphysical in the text and reading them as functional descriptions of contemplative experience. This is a defensible interpretive move and also one that some traditional readers find disrespectful. The text genuinely does take past and future lives literally. Treating that as a metaphor for self-knowledge is a translation, and translations have politics. I am noting the choice rather than hiding it.</SideNote>

## What the five yamas share

A pattern is visible across the five. Each names a specific cognitive load — threat-monitoring (*ahiṃsā*), parallel-model maintenance (*satya*), distributional anxiety (*asteya*), reward-system pursuit (*brahmacarya*), craving (*aparigraha*). Each load is paid for in autonomic arousal and attentional bandwidth. Each is, by Patañjali's account, sufficiently severe that the later limbs cannot do their work on a substrate carrying it.

The five together are not exhaustive. There are other cognitive loads that prevent contemplative training — chronic resentment is one, identity-defense is another, future-rehearsal is a third — and the yamas list does not name them directly. The classical list captures the loads Patañjali considered most universal and most blocking. A modern reformulation might include others. But the structure of the list, and the function it performs in the system, would remain.

## What this reading commits us to

If the structural reading is right, three things follow that have implications for contemporary contemplative practice.

First, the yamas are not optional. They are not "a list of nice things to do *if* you want to be a good person, before getting on with the meditation." They are the first step of the meditation. A practice that ignores them is a practice operating on a noisy substrate, regardless of how dedicated the meditation cushion time is.

Second, the yamas are testable. The hypothesis — that the loads they name are real, that reducing those loads improves the substrate's readiness for the later limbs — is, in principle, falsifiable. To my knowledge, no controlled trial has used yama-niyama interventions as the manipulation and downstream meditation depth as the outcome. The hypothesis is implicit in every traditional manual and has not been put to a modern test.<Cite id="van-dam-2018" /> If anyone reading this has run, or knows of, such a study, please tell me.

Third, the structural reading reframes the moral psychology. The yamas, on this account, are not virtues to be cultivated for their own sake. They are clinical interventions on the substrate, and the moral content is — at least partly — downstream. A practitioner who refrains from harm because of the autonomic costs has done the same structural work as one who refrains from harm because of moral conviction. The motivations differ; the substrate effects do not. Whether this should comfort or trouble us is a separate question. The framework, taken seriously, predicts that ethics-from-contemplation and ethics-from-moral-conviction converge on similar behavior through different routes, which is a claim modern moral psychology could probably do something with.
