## Prologue: two vocabularies, one nervous system

Patañjali compiled the *Yoga Sūtras* around 400 BCE.<SideNote>The date is contested. Estimates range from 200 BCE to 400 CE, and the question of whether "Patañjali" refers to a single author or a redactor synthesizing earlier material is unresolved. Edwin Bryant's introduction (<Cite id="bryant-2009" />) surveys the scholarly fight; the upshot is that we are reading a compilation, possibly across several hands, of practices already old when they were written down. None of this changes what the text *says*; it changes who said it.</SideNote> He had no MRI, no Vienna, and as far as we know no opinions about the prefrontal cortex. He had practitioners, a method of introspection refined across centuries, and the patience to write down what he found. The result is a book of roughly 196 aphorisms so compressed that traditional Sanskrit commentaries are longer than the source text by a factor of about twenty, and modern English commentaries are longer than the traditional ones, and you, gentle reader, are currently inside the fourth commentary I have written this year.

The text the *Sūtras* opens onto is a model of mind. Not a metaphysics, although there is metaphysics in it. Not a religion, although it has been folded into several. A model — a working description of what minds do, what trained minds do differently, and what the limit case of a trained mind looks like from the inside. Patañjali's model is, in places, structurally testable by instruments he could not have imagined. In other places it makes claims no scan will ever settle. The essay you are reading is an attempt to take the model seriously, mark where the imaging supports it, mark where it points beyond what imaging can do, and resist the temptation to inflate the first into the second.

This is harder than it sounds. The contemplative-neuroscience literature has had a rough two decades. Early imaging studies on meditators generated headlines about "rewiring the brain" and were quickly absorbed into a wellness industrial complex that promised, on the basis of small studies with weak controls and self-selected populations, that eight weeks of mindfulness would do everything from curing depression to reversing cellular aging. Then came the corrections. The most influential is Van Dam and colleagues' 2018 paper "Mind the Hype," <Cite id="van-dam-2018" /> which is exactly what it sounds like — a pointed catalogue of the methodological problems that had accumulated under the field's surface.<SideNote>Their list is not flattering. Definitional ambiguity (what counts as "mindfulness"). Definitional drift across studies. Small samples. Self-selection. Lack of active control conditions. Failure to document adverse effects. Publication bias. Media amplification of preliminary findings. The authors' stated goal is "to minimize harm, curb poor research practices, and staunch the flow of misinformation." Honest criticism of a literature that needed it.</SideNote> The honest contemplative neuroscientist now writes with one hand on the keyboard and the other on Van Dam et al., as ballast.

So this essay carries ballast. Every substantive claim is tagged for the level of evidence behind it. The taxonomy has three terms:

**Evidenced** — the claim is supported by replicated empirical findings, in studies with reasonable methodology. The interoceptive pathway from vagus through nucleus tractus solitarius to insula is evidenced. The reliable increase in heart rate variability under slow paced breathing is evidenced. The cortical thickening of right anterior insula in long-term meditators is evidenced, with the caveat that the studies are mostly cross-sectional and the samples are mostly small.

**Suggestive** — the claim is consistent with current data but rests on weaker foundations. Small samples, contested mechanisms, or analogical inference. Polyvagal theory's specific evolutionary claims are suggestive. The mapping of dhyāna onto default-mode-network quieting is suggestive (the imaging is there, the practitioners' self-reports are there, but the inference that one is the substrate of the other is partly an act of translation).

**Philosophical** — the claim is best held as a way of speaking about experience; no scan will settle it. The Advaita witness (*sākṣī*) is philosophical. Whether reality is ultimately one or two is philosophical. The hard problem of consciousness is, as far as anyone can currently tell, *constitutively* philosophical — not philosophical because we haven't figured it out yet but philosophical in a way no future neuroscientific result will dissolve.<SideNote>This is the framing in <Cite id="chalmers-1995" />, which introduced the term "hard problem" and named the gap between explaining functions (what Chalmers calls the easy problems) and explaining experience (the hard one). Thirty years on, the hard problem is exactly as hard as Chalmers said it was, and the contemplative traditions have something to offer the discussion that they did not have in 1995 — populations of practitioners who have spent decades in the state-space the philosophers are arguing about.</SideNote>

The tags are not hedges. They are the editorial discipline that keeps this kind of writing honest. When the prose says "evidenced" and points to a real paper, you can check the paper. When the prose says "philosophical," you should not pretend the evidenced claims are doing the work the philosophical ones aspire to. The two vocabularies — Patañjali's and the neuroscience's — speak in different registers, and the seam between them is what this essay is about.

A small note on the voice. This is going to be a long essay, and I would like you to actually read it, so I will let it breathe where it wants to breathe. There will be sentences that exist primarily to make a small point. There will, very occasionally, be jokes. The jokes will earn their place by tightening a claim or by puncturing a sanctimony that the material is otherwise prone to. The model is Oliver Sacks in his careful moods: rigorous, warm, and entirely willing to admit when he does not know.

## Awareness is layered, not unitary

The single most important conceptual move in this essay is reframing what the eight limbs are. The conventional reading treats them as stages on a ladder: master the first, climb to the second, and so on, until samādhi at the top. This reading is not wrong, exactly. It is what the traditional sequence suggests if you read it in a hurry. It is also what teachers reach for when they want to motivate the work — *first you have to live ethically, then you can practice posture, then you can regulate the breath, and so on.* Useful as a pedagogical fiction. Misleading as a description of what is actually going on.

The more useful reading is that the eight limbs are *layers*. They name eight strata of the nervous-system substrate that produce awareness, and each layer must be in some workable state — not perfect, just workable — for the next layer's work to be possible. The yamas and niyamas don't lift you to āsana; they make a nervous system available for āsana. Āsana doesn't lift you to prāṇāyāma; it settles the body so the breath has somewhere quiet to be regulated. And so on up. The whole stack is cultivated in parallel, with the upper layers stabilizing as the lower ones consolidate.<SideNote>This reframing is not original; Edwin Bryant and several traditional commentators are clear that the limbs are not a strict sequence. What is closer to original here is the attempt to ground the parallel reading in the layered architecture of the nervous system that modern neuroscience has independently described. The eight-limb stack and the network hierarchy of consciousness are not the same picture, but they are pictures of the same shape.</SideNote>

Modern neuroscience independently arrived at a similar layering, though it described it differently and for different reasons. The level of consciousness — are you awake, drowsy, asleep, in a coma — is one thing. The contents of consciousness — what are you experiencing right now — is another. The level depends on brainstem and thalamic systems, the famous ascending reticular activating system; the contents depend on cortex. You can damage one and leave the other intact. This is the structural lesson of decades of work on coma, persistent vegetative states, anesthesia, and sleep.<SideNote>The clearest synthesis of this two-system architecture is in <Cite id="dehaene-2014" />, especially chapter 5 on the neural correlates of consciousness. The level/contents distinction is older than Dehaene's framing — it goes back to mid-20th-century work on the reticular formation by Moruzzi and Magoun — but Dehaene's book is the place to start.</SideNote>

Ned Block sharpened this further in 1995 with a distinction philosophy of mind has been arguing about ever since: phenomenal consciousness versus access consciousness.<Cite id="block-1995" /> Phenomenal consciousness (P-consciousness) is the felt quality of experience, the redness of red, the throb of a stubbed toe. Access consciousness (A-consciousness) is the availability of mental content for reasoning, report, and rational control. Block argued that these can come apart. There can be P without A — phenomenal experience that is not available for verbal report (the famous "I just noticed I'd been hearing the refrigerator for an hour" experience). Whether there can be A without P is the much-contested question; one's answer to it correlates closely with one's stance on whether sophisticated AI systems are conscious.<SideNote>The standard interlocutors here are Block himself, Stanislas Dehaene (a P/A skeptic — he tends to think the distinction collapses on closer inspection), and Hakwan Lau (a higher-order theorist who thinks A is, in the end, what matters). The COGITATE adversarial collaboration (<Cite id="cogitate-2023" />) was in part a test of whether the imaging signature of consciousness lives in the late, broadcasting frontal-parietal "ignition" that GWT predicts (an A-consciousness signature) or in the earlier, more localized posterior activity that IIT predicts (which Block has long argued tracks P-consciousness). The 2023 results were genuinely mixed; neither theory walked away cleanly.</SideNote>

The reason this matters for Patañjali is that the eight limbs are not all about the same thing. Some are training A-consciousness — the deliberate selection and sustaining of content for awareness. Dhāraṇā, one-pointedness, is straightforwardly an A-consciousness practice. You are training the ability to keep a specific content available for awareness over time. Cognitive neuroscience has a long and detailed account of how this works, what it costs, and what changes in people who practice it.

Other limbs are working on something different. Prāṇāyāma changes what the body is sending up the interoceptive pipeline; what arrives in cortex is different not because it has been more skillfully selected but because the upstream signal has been modulated. This is closer to a P-consciousness intervention — a change in the texture of felt experience itself.<SideNote>One would not, in mid-prāṇāyāma, say "the contents of awareness are now different." One would say "the breath feels different, the body feels different, attention itself feels different." The intervention is on the felt quality, not on the selection. P-consciousness, in Block's terms; the felt body in Damasio's. Different vocabulary, same target.</SideNote> Samādhi, at the limit, is doing yet another thing — not selecting better content, not modulating the felt quality, but quieting the apparatus that normally constructs the sense of being a subject distinct from content at all. Three different operations on three different parts of the awareness stack.

The third great framework for thinking about the layered nervous system, alongside the level/contents split and the P/A split, is **predictive processing**.<Cite id="friston-2010" /> The brain on this view is not a passive receiver of information. It is an organ that is constantly *predicting* what its inputs will be, comparing predictions to incoming signal, and updating predictions to minimize prediction error. Perception is not "what your senses tell you"; perception is the brain's best guess about what is causing your sensory input, refined moment by moment by the error between guess and signal. Anil Seth calls this account "the controlled hallucination," and he means it literally: ordinary perception is a hallucination that happens to be tightly constrained by the world.<Cite id="seth-2021" />

The predictive-processing account of *interoception* is the part of this framework most relevant to what follows. Older accounts treated interoception as a kind of passive perception — the body sends signals up, the brain receives them, the felt body is the result. The predictive-processing version is more interesting and considerably weirder. The brain is predicting what the body will be doing, those predictions are compared to incoming signal, and the felt body is the *prediction*, updated by error.<Cite id="seth-friston-2016" /> What you feel is not your body. What you feel is your brain's best guess about your body, which is to say a model that has been shaped by every past body-state and is currently being corrected (or not) by the signal coming in.

This sounds abstract until you notice that it explains, in one move, why prāṇāyāma works. Slow breathing is not magic and is not, in any mystical sense, "energy work." It is an active manipulation of one of the strongest, most stable interoceptive signals the brain has access to. The breath is something the body cannot help doing and that conscious attention can directly shape — almost the only such signal. Modulate the breath and you modulate one of the major streams of prediction error feeding into the felt body. The model updates. The felt body changes.<SideNote>This is, I think, the cleanest way to translate between the yogic vocabulary of *prāṇa* and the modern neuroscience of interoception. The traditional language describes prāṇāyāma as working on the subtle body through subtle channels (*nāḍī*). The modern language describes it as changing the prediction-error landscape of the interoceptive system. These are different descriptions of the same operation. The traditional language is phenomenologically precise but anatomically wrong. The modern language is anatomically right but phenomenologically thin. The point is not to choose one. The point is to recognize that both are pointing at the same thing from opposite sides.</SideNote>

The layering picture, then, is this. Wakefulness comes from the brainstem and thalamus and is mostly unconscious in the sense that you cannot voluntarily modulate it without the rest of the system going haywire. The contents of waking awareness are constructed by cortex, in an active predictive process that integrates body signals (interoception) with world signals (exteroception) and prior expectations. Specific contents are selected and sustained by attentional networks — the executive control network, primarily — at metabolic cost. The sense of being someone, located in a body, observing this content, is a separate construction by a different set of networks (default mode, salience, temporoparietal junction, insula). Each of these layers is real, each is partially trainable, and each is the target of one or more of the eight limbs. The framework is layered all the way down.

## The ethical substrate: yamas and niyamas, and why putting them first was not arbitrary

Here is the part most likely to be misread, and I would like to spend some care on it.

The five *yamas* — *ahiṃsā* (non-harming), *satya* (truthfulness), *asteya* (non-stealing), *brahmacarya* (sensory restraint), and *aparigraha* (non-grasping) — and the five *niyamas* — *śauca* (purity), *santoṣa* (contentment), *tapas* (disciplined effort), *svādhyāya* (self-study), and *īśvara-praṇidhāna* (surrender to a divine, however construed) — appear first in Patañjali's eight-limb list. The standard reading takes them as ethical preliminaries: get your life in order, then you can practice yoga. This is fine as far as it goes. It is also, I think, missing the structural point.

The yamas and niyamas are best read not as a moral code but as a list of **cognitive loads** that prevent contemplative training, paired with a list of **orientations** that facilitate it. Patañjali noticed, over many practitioners and many years, that minds tangled in certain conditions could not productively do the work the later limbs require. He wrote down what he observed. The result looks like a moral system, and it has been treated as one, but its structural function is closer to a clinical instrument — a checklist of what to clear before you can do the procedure.<SideNote>Calling it a "clinical instrument" is, of course, anachronistic. Patañjali was not a clinician; the conceptual category did not exist. But the structural function the yamas serve in his system is much closer to what we would now call a screening tool — a way of identifying whether the substrate is ready — than to what we would now call ethics. Treating it as ethics makes it sound optional ("a list of nice things to do"); treating it as a clinical instrument makes its structural priority obvious ("you cannot do the procedure on a noisy substrate").</SideNote>

Take *satya* first, because it is the cleanest case. The cognitive cost of lying is well-documented. fMRI studies of deception consistently show recruitment of prefrontal regions beyond what truthful responses require — the anterior cingulate, the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, parts of the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex.<SideNote>The deception-imaging literature is large and has its own methodological controversies (see the various critiques of "fMRI lie detection" attempts in the early 2000s). The robust finding underneath the controversy is straightforward: lying is *harder* than telling the truth, in a way that shows up reliably in reaction times and in additional cortical engagement. The hard question is what to do with that finding in real-world contexts (you cannot accurately classify a single instance of lying from a brain scan). For our purposes, the relevant fact is the cost, not the detectability.</SideNote> Lying is measurably slower than truth-telling. Sustained deception over weeks or months produces a kind of executive-function depletion — the liar is, in a meaningful sense, carrying additional cognitive weight just to maintain the lie's coherence with the rest of their reported life.

Patañjali was not running brain scans. But he was watching practitioners, and he noticed that a mind tangled in deception could not settle into sustained attention. The prescription was not "lying is bad" — it was "lying is expensive, and the practice cannot afford the cost." A mind that is concurrently maintaining two parallel models of reality, and adjudicating between them in real time about which one to share with each interlocutor, does not have the spare capacity to do dhāraṇā. The yama is structural.

The same pattern repeats for the others. *Ahiṃsā* is famously translated as non-harming, but the structural cost it addresses is sustained vigilance. Real harm produces real consequences — the harmed party may retaliate, the action may be discovered, the harm may need to be concealed or rationalized. All of this requires threat-monitoring, which the sympathetic system pays for in arousal and which attention pays for in bandwidth. *Aparigraha* — non-grasping — addresses what addiction research now describes in detail: chronic craving installs sustained activity in the ventral striatum and persistent demands on prefrontal control. The brain is not at rest; it is in a state of low-grade pursuit. *Asteya* and *brahmacarya* are variations on the same theme. Each yama identifies a different way the substrate can be made noisy, and each prescription is the systematic reduction of that noise.

The niyamas do something complementary. Where the yamas remove load, the niyamas add orientation. *Santoṣa* — contentment — names a particular relation to current conditions: the cultivated capacity to be at peace with what is, without that peace depending on what is being any particular way. Acceptance-based therapies have studied this construct for forty years and find that it is associated with reduced sympathetic arousal, reduced rumination, and better outcomes on a range of mental-health measures.<SideNote>The clinical evidence base for acceptance-based interventions (ACT, MBSR-style mindfulness, dialectical behavior therapy in some of its components) is, broadly, the strongest in the broader contemplative-clinical space. It is also exactly the literature most vulnerable to Van Dam et al.'s critique — many of the foundational studies are small, the active control conditions are weak, and the effect sizes shrink under stricter methodology. The honest position is: there is something here, the literature has overstated it, and the something is real enough that the overstatement does not invalidate the underlying observation.</SideNote> *Svādhyāya* — self-study — names metacognition, the capacity to observe one's own mind. Metacognition has a reasonably well-characterized neural substrate, principally in frontopolar cortex and the posterior medial frontal cortex, and training that improves it produces measurable changes there. *Tapas* — disciplined effort, literally "heat" — names the willingness to do the work when the work is uncomfortable; the executive control network does the carrying. *Īśvara-praṇidhāna* — surrender to a divine — is the niyama hardest for secular readers. Its structural function is the relinquishing of the felt sense of being the manager of one's own life; the grasping after control is itself a cognitive load, and letting it go (by surrendering to a divine, or to the practice, or simply to what is) reduces the load.

The structural reading does something useful: it makes the yamas and niyamas testable, at least in principle. The hypothesis is no longer "Patañjali had good values"; the hypothesis is "the loads named by the yamas, and the orientations named by the niyamas, are reliably associated with measurable differences in the substrate the later limbs work on." That is a falsifiable claim, and to my knowledge it has not been formally tested.<SideNote>I would be very glad to be wrong about this. If anyone reading has run, or knows of, a controlled trial in which yama-niyama interventions were the manipulation and downstream meditation depth (or one of its plausible proxies — sustained attention task performance, DMN-quieting under standardized meditation, interoceptive accuracy) was the outcome, please tell me. The literature I can find takes the yamas and niyamas as background context to mindfulness interventions, not as the manipulation itself. The hypothesis remains untested.</SideNote> The contemplative literature implies it everywhere; the clinical literature has not, to my knowledge, designed a study to evaluate it.

This is the most distinctive editorial reading in this essay, and it is also where I want to be most explicit about the seam. I am claiming that the first two limbs of an ancient framework can be productively reread through the lens of modern cognitive load theory and the cognitive cost of sustained deception, harm, and craving. The rereading is structurally plausible and lines up with what is known about the costs of those states. It is not what Patañjali says explicitly. He says "do not lie, do not harm, do not grasp." The structural interpretation is mine, not his.

But here is the thing. The structural interpretation makes sense of an otherwise strange feature of the text — the placement of these particular five-and-five at the head of an otherwise psychological list. If the yamas and niyamas were simply ethics, their placement at the start of a contemplative manual would be a moral preface to an otherwise unrelated practice. Read structurally, they are not a preface. They are the first step in the actual procedure: clear these loads and cultivate these orientations, because the rest of the procedure operates on a substrate that needs to be quiet.

Two and a half millennia later, modern cognitive neuroscience has, working entirely on its own problems, independently identified the same set of loads. That convergence may not constitute proof of Patañjali's claim. But it is the kind of consistency that, in other fields, gets called a clue.
